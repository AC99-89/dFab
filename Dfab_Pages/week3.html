Step 4: Remodeling the gears

The first thing I decided to do was to remodel the gears because I had an extremely big design flaw in my previous gears. The problem with the old gears was that they would not mesh with the circular rack gear. To fix this, I did two things: I changed my mindset by trying to make a planetary gear system, and having fifty one teeth on my inner ring (circular rack) instead of thirty nine.

Why did I do such a thing?

Great that you asked! I changed my mindset to a planetary gear system because it made more sense. My mechanism was based on moving little gear around a ring by powering a motor on a big gear which is literally what a planetary gear system is. The reason why I changed the amount of teeth in my rack is due to the planetary gear mesh formula. The formula states the teeth on the inner ring (my circular rack) must equal the amount of teeth on my sun gear (my 27 teeth gear) + 2 * the amount of teeth on my driven gear (the 12 teeth gear). My problem is that 39 does not equal 27 + 2* 12; the formula equals 51! Therefore, I added 18 extra teeth on my gear to satisfy the formula. I did those remodifications and my gears work way better! I also made a square 4x4 mm hole in my sun gear to fit my axle in perfectly.

I laser cutted the parts but the legs didn't work because I didn't account for the thickness. Instead, I decided to drill two holes into my main base and screw in two m40 screws through the motor and the platform and that managed to keep my motor in place!

Step 19: Complete The Circuit

I get an Arduino Uno and an L9110 and hook up my motor to the pin 3 of my Arduino Uno and to 1-A of the L9110. I then hooked up to 5v, the GNDs, and VCC. Then, I hooked my potentiometer to A0, A2, and A4 of my Arduino Uno.

Step 20: Program The Microcontroller

Then, I went to the professor's website and copy and pasted the potentiometer motor code into the Arduino Uno. My project can now be sped up or down by a potentiometer! We can now say that my kinetic sculpture is finished! Code is shown below.

  /*  Code to run motor in one direction based on potentiometer reading.
 *  Motor driver speed control on pin 3 (direction LOW by default)
 *  Potentiometer plugged into A0, A2, and A4 on Arduino Uno (or similar).
 */

void setup() {
  pinMode(3, OUTPUT);
  pinMode(A0, OUTPUT);  //This will be GND for the potentiometer
  pinMode(A4, OUTPUT);  //This will be 3.3V for the pot

  digitalWrite(A0, LOW);
  digitalWrite(A4, HIGH);
}

void loop() {
  int pot_value = analogRead(A2);   //pot wiper is on A2
  int motor_speed = map(pot_value, 0, 1023, 255, 0); // map motor_level to pot_value so that zero corresponds to lowest speed
  analogWrite(3, motor_speed);
  delay(1);
}

Assignment 2: Measuring A Circuit's Voltage Using A Multimeter

I decided to measure a motor's amps using Ohm's law, but first I need to find the voltage and resistance of the motor using a multimeter. A multimeter is used to measure the voltage and resistance of a circuit, so it was the go-to tool. I measured my voltage to be 3.16 (after hooking it up to bench power and using 3.16 volts) and my resistance was 3 (on 2000 option).
Before we can use Ohm's law, you need to know how it works first.

Ohm's law is used to calculate the amount of Amps in the current, the resistance, or volts depending on how you set the formula. In class, we used I= V/R (I is current in amps, V is voltage, and R is resistance). I will be using this version of the formula to caluclate my current in amps.

I placed my variables in my equation; my formula is now I = 3.16 / 3. After solving it, I got 1.05 as my current in amps.
